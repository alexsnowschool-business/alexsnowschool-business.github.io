# When estimates can't keep up with desire

*A parade of nine-figure surprises reveals collectors chasing undervalued modernists with newfound conviction.*

---

This week's six overperformers reveal a market that skewed sharply toward Christie's—five of six sales crossed the block there—and favored underestimated works across a narrow price band: five hammered below $115,000. The disparities were striking. A Sluijters portrait initially pegged at $3,000 closed at nearly $31,000; a modest Kuwata ceramic nearly matched a six-figure Signac in percentage terms. The estimate, in theory, reflects a specialist's calibrated read on collector appetite and comparable sales. When results deviate this far—most by over 750 percent—it signals either conservative preliminary positioning or a meaningful disconnect between pre-sale expectations and actual bidding dynamics. Whether the gap reflects undervaluation, fresh money in the room, or category-specific momentum remains the operative question.

---

The week's outperformers clustered in decorative arts and design—categories that have historically tracked economic sentiment closely. As we head into spring sales season, monitor whether this momentum sustains across both houses. Strength here often signals collector appetite beyond blue-chip paintings, suggesting a market broadening rather than deepening. That shift, if it holds, reshapes where value accrues across the entire ecosystem.
