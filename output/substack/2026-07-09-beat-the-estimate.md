# Rodin, Dürer, and the tyranny of conservative estimates

*When auction houses underprice across centuries and mediums, collectors vote decisively with their wallets.*

---

This week's overperformers cluster almost entirely within a single Sotheby's sale, with six works spanning Old Master prints, modern sculpture, rare books, and aviation ephemera all clearing their low estimates by 333 to 367 percent. The concentration itself is instructive: rather than signal broad market momentum, it suggests a specialist sale where estimates were set conservatively—or where the assembled bidders held materially different views of value than the house's initial read. When a Rodin and a Dürer print trade at near-identical percentage premiums alongside a Valéry binding and an unsigned aircraft illustration, the pattern points less to category-wide strength than to a particular room's appetite on a particular afternoon. Estimate-beating matters precisely because estimates represent a specialist's calibrated guess at market consensus. Wide gaps don't prove undervaluation; they register disagreement. This sale registered significant disagreement.

The consistent performance across this week's Sotheby's sales suggests collectors remain disciplined even as market signals turn mixed. Watch closely how secondary-market pricing stabilizes in coming weeks—particularly whether mid-tier lots maintain momentum or retreat. The real indicator will be sell-through rates on less-marquee material, where conviction typically shows first.
