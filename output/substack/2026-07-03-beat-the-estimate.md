# Aivazovsky's Venice steals the show in blockbuster week

*Academic painters are suddenly hot again, with collectors bidding aggressively on 19th-century European salon favorites.*

---

This week's overperformers clustered almost entirely within Sotheby's Old Masters sales, with five works across nineteenth-century European painting clearing their low estimates by an average of 473 percent. The concentration matters: it suggests not market-wide enthusiasm but rather specialist-driven mispricing within a specific category—late Romantic and academic painting, primarily. When estimates miss this badly, it signals a gap between the auction house's conservative positioning and actual collector demand in the room. The estimates themselves represent the specialist's thesis about where the market sits; a 620 percent gap, as seen with Hughes's "Waning Moon," indicates either a deliberate lowball strategy or a genuine surprise in bidder appetite. Understanding why the room disagreed with the house's read—whether through fresh money, newly fashionable artists, or simple supply scarcity—matters more than the spread itself. The data here points to a specific pocket of the market that the estimates failed to anticipate.

## 1. Edward Robert Hughes — Waning Moon

**Sotheby's** · 19Th 20Th Century European Art Auction L26230  
Estimate: $30,000–$50,000 · Hammer: **$215,900** (620% above low estimate)

Edward Robert Hughes's Pre-Raphaelite moonlit reverie exposed a significant blind spot in Sotheby's valuation. The Victorian narrative painter, whose meticulous jewel-toned compositions have gained traction among collectors recalibrating Victorian art beyond the canonical names, commanded prices that suggest estimators are still calibrating demand for second-tier Pre-Raphaelites. "Waning Moon" exemplifies Hughes's signature technique of rendering nocturnal scenes with crystalline detail—here, a woman silhouetted against luminous night sky—a subject matter now driving premium valuations.
