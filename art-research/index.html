History, Philosophy & the Sociology of the Auction

Part I

A History of Western Art

Twelve moments from 35,000 BCE to the present — each a rupture, not an evolution. Art history is not a smooth progression of improving technique; it is a succession of breaks, each one answering a social, philosophical, or political crisis that the previous mode could no longer contain.

c. 35,000 BCE
The Cave and the Hand

The Lascaux and Chauvet cave paintings are not decoration. The hand stencils at Cueva de las Manos (Argentina, 9,000 BCE) record a presence — a mark that says: I was here. The auroch and horse painted in ochre and manganese are not documentation of what hunters saw; they precede the hunt. Image-making is performative, a participation in what is depicted. This is the oldest version of the question that art history never resolves: does the representation affect the reality?

Upper Palaeolithic · Image as performance
500–323 BCE
Classical Greece: Ideal and Canon

Polykleitos writes the Kanon — a treatise setting the proportional ratios of the ideal human body. The Doryphoros (Spear-Bearer, c. 440 BCE) is its physical embodiment. Greek sculpture does not represent individual bodies; it represents the body as it should be. This is the first explicit theorisation of art as normative: the work proposes a standard. The Western tradition of figurative painting and sculpture — still visible in Christie's lots featuring academic figure work — descends directly from this premise.

Classical Period · Idealisation as politics
1420–1527
The Renaissance: Perspective and the Autonomous Artist

Brunelleschi demonstrates linear perspective around 1420 — a mathematical system for representing three-dimensional space on a flat surface. This is not merely a technique; it repositions the viewer as the fixed, rational subject at the centre of a coherent spatial world. Simultaneously, the artist begins to emerge as an individual with a biography and a recognisable style: Vasari's Lives of the Artists (1550) invents art history as a story of great men. The workshop system gives way to the named master. The Medici become the first patrons whose taste shapes a programme rather than simply commissioning devotional objects.

Early Modernity · Perspective, biography, patronage
1600–1750
Baroque: Drama, Light, and Counter-Reformation

Caravaggio's tenebrism — extreme chiaroscuro, figures pulled from darkness into sudden light — is a rhetorical instrument of the Counter-Reformation. The aim: visceral conviction, not elegant instruction. The church needs painting that grabs the gut, not the intellect. In the Northern tradition, Dutch Golden Age painting serves a new client: the merchant bourgeoisie. Still-life vanitas paintings encode Calvinist theology inside images of wealth. Rembrandt's portraits record the specific, mortal individual — wrinkles, asymmetry — as opposed to the Renaissance idealised type.

Counter-Reformation · Dutch bourgeoisie · Mortality
1820–1900
Romanticism to Impressionism: Feeling Against System

The Romantics reject Enlightenment rationalism: Goya's Black Paintings (1819–23) are not beautiful. Géricault's Raft of the Medusa (1819) refuses to depict the heroic male nude — it depicts drowning, despair, a government scandal. In 1874 a group of rejected painters exhibit outside the official Salon: Monet, Renoir, Degas, Pissarro. The Impressionists paint sensation rather than subject — the shimmer of light on water, not the object itself. It is the first move toward abstraction: the image begins to be about the act of perceiving rather than the thing perceived.

Post-Revolution · Industrialisation · Salon rejection
1885–1920
Post-Impressionism: Structure and Emotion

Cézanne dismantles perspective — his apples sit on multiple planes simultaneously, as if the eye is moving rather than fixed. This is not a failure; it is a programme. He is painting the act of looking. Van Gogh uses colour and impasto not to describe but to express an interior state. Gauguin abandons Europe for Tahiti and begins primitivism — a problematic but consequential move: non-Western visual traditions enter the Western art discourse as resources (and as objects of cultural appropriation). Cézanne is the hinge: without him, Cubism and all subsequent abstraction is impossible.

Pre-WWI · Cézanne as rupture
1905–1925
Fauvism, Cubism, Expressionism: Three Breaks

Matisse and the Fauves use colour freed from descriptive function — pure chromatic sensation. Picasso and Braque's Cubism (1907–14) fragments the object into simultaneous viewpoints: the figure seen from front and side at once. Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907) borrows from Iberian and African masks, introducing a structural challenge to Western pictorial illusionism that comes from outside Europe. In Germany, Kirchner and the Brücke artists use distortion, angularity, and raw colour to depict the alienation of urban modernity — the city as wound.

Avant-garde formations · WWI rupture ahead
1916–1945
Dada and Surrealism: Against Reason

Dada (Zurich, 1916) is art's response to WWI: if civilisation produced the Somme, then civilisation's logic — including aesthetic logic — is worthless. The readymade (Duchamp's Fountain, 1917) is the most consequential gesture in 20th-century art: a urinal submitted to a jury. It removes the artist's hand entirely and asks only: what is art? Who decides? Surrealism inherits Dada's assault on rational order but channels it through Freudian dream logic — automatism, the uncanny, desire. Dalí, Ernst, Kahlo. The unconscious as subject matter.

Post-WWI trauma · Freud · Anti-bourgeois
1945–1965
Abstract Expressionism: The New York School

Pollock pours enamel paint onto canvas laid on the floor. De Kooning tears and pastes and repaints. Rothko builds atmospheric fields of colour. This is the movement most heavily represented in the Christie's dataset: Frankenthaler ($9.3M total), Pollock ($7.7M), Mitchell ($5.4M), Motherwell ($2.7M). The critical context matters: Harold Rosenberg's "action painting" — the canvas as arena for an act — and Clement Greenberg's formalist account of the medium's self-purification. The Cold War dimension is real: the CIA funded exhibitions of Abstract Expressionism as evidence of American cultural freedom versus Soviet Socialist Realism.

Post-WWII · New York displaces Paris · Cold War politics
1955–1975
Pop Art: The Mass Image as Subject

Warhol silkscreens Campbell's soup cans and Marilyn Monroe in lurid series. Lichtenstein blows up comic-book panels. Ruscha paints gas stations and word-images. The move is twofold: first, the erasure of the distinction between high and commercial culture (Greenberg's formalist hierarchy inverted); second, the introduction of seriality and mechanical reproduction as style. For Warhol, the artwork's "aura" (Benjamin) is not something to mourn but to celebrate and exploit: fame is the content. The Christie's dataset includes Warhol ($1.5M total) and Ruscha ($4.6M, 9 lots) — both command reliable institutional prices.

Consumer society · Seriality · High/low collapse
1960–1985
Minimalism & Conceptual Art: Object and Idea

Frank Stella's shaped canvases strip the painting to its literal object — what you see is what you see. Judd's "specific objects" are neither painting nor sculpture: they occupy space and refuse metaphor. Conceptual art (Sol LeWitt, Joseph Kosuth) takes this further: the artwork is the idea; the physical realisation is incidental. Sol LeWitt's wall drawings can be executed by anyone following instructions. This creates the condition for the contemporary art market's central paradox: the most "dematerialised" works (Conceptual, NFTs) often command the highest prices, because the idea of the work is fully controlled by the artist's name — the ultimate brand.

Post-Abstract Expressionism · Literalism · Dematerialisation
1980–Present
Neo-Expressionism, the Market, and the Present

The 1980s art market boom produces Neo-Expressionism: Basquiat, Schnabel, Salle. Raw painting returns as a reaction against Conceptual distance. The auction house becomes the primary price-discovery mechanism and the primary publicity engine: a Basquiat sold at auction is news in the same registers as a sporting record. By 2010 the market bifurcates dramatically: ultra-high-end (billion-dollar Basquiats) vs. everything else. The Christie's dataset confirms this: 55% of lots sell above estimate, but the distribution is extremely skewed — Longo's Study of Eric sells at 14.7× estimate while many academic works sell at or below.

Market boom · Neo-expressionism · Global collecting
Part II

Four Frameworks for Understanding Art

These four thinkers offer the most structurally durable tools for analysing what art is, how it acquires value, and why the auction market works the way the data shows it does.

Immanuel Kant
1724–1804
Critique of Judgement · Disinterested Pleasure

Kant's Critique of the Power of Judgement (1790) establishes the philosophical foundations for modern aesthetics. Aesthetic judgement, he argues, is a claim that something is beautiful — not merely that it pleases me (which would be a report of personal preference) but that it should please everyone. This "universal communicability without a concept" is what distinguishes the aesthetic from the merely agreeable or the good. The beauty of a Rothko is not reducible to its price, its function, or its cultural prestige. Kant calls the proper mode of engaging beauty "disinterested" — which does not mean indifferent, but uncontaminated by desire, use, or ownership.

"Taste is the faculty of estimating an object or a mode of representation by means of a delight or an aversion apart from any interest."
Applied to the Dataset
The auction market is structurally Kantian in its vocabulary ("timeless," "significant," "important") while being anti-Kantian in its mechanics. A lot that sells at 14.7× estimate is not more beautiful than one at 0.8×. The disinterested aesthetic and the financial transaction occupy the same room and pretend not to see each other.
Walter Benjamin
1892–1940
Aura · Mechanical Reproduction · Authenticity

Benjamin's "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction" (1935) is the most widely cited text in art criticism for good reason: it identifies a structural transformation. The "aura" of a work of art — its here-and-now, its embeddedness in tradition, its unique presence in a particular place — is eroded by mechanical reproduction. A photograph of the Mona Lisa circulates globally; the original gathers crowds who are partly there to confirm that the original exists. Benjamin's thesis is ambivalent: the destruction of aura democratises art (film, photography reach millions) but also removes the basis for the work's ritual authority — which the art market immediately reconstructs around uniqueness and provenance.

"That which withers in the age of mechanical reproduction is the aura of the work of art."
Applied to the Dataset
Aura is what the auction house sells. "Property from a Distinguished Private Collection" restores ritual authority through provenance. The Pollock hammer price ($3.8M) does not reflect the visual experience of the work alone — it reflects the certified uniqueness of this canvas with this history. That is Benjamin's aura reconstructed as financial instrument.
Clement Greenberg
1909–1994
Formalism · Medium Specificity · Avant-Garde vs Kitsch

Greenberg's "Avant-Garde and Kitsch" (1939) draws the battle line: serious art resists being made easy; kitsch is the academicisation of the avant-garde's surface effects for mass consumption. His formalist criticism insists that each medium must explore and foreground its own specific qualities — painting must be about flatness, colour, and the picture plane, not illusion. This is why he championed the Abstract Expressionists and Colour Field painters (Frankenthaler is directly connected — she poured stain onto raw canvas, discovering the medium's absorptive materiality). He opposed Pop Art precisely because it reinstated representational content and cultural reference that he saw as retrograde.

"The avant-garde's principal function has been to keep culture moving in the midst of ideological confusion and violence."
Applied to the Dataset
The market does not follow Greenberg's hierarchy: Ruscha (whom Greenberg would have dismissed as kitsch-adjacent Pop) commands $4.6M across 9 lots. Frankenthaler ($9.3M, 6 lots) is the formalist champion in the dataset but is also the market's top performer. The data accidentally confirms that aesthetic prestige and market performance are not uncorrelated — but the mechanism is institutional reputation, not formal purity.
Pierre Bourdieu
1930–2002
Cultural Capital · Field Theory · Distinction

Bourdieu's Distinction (1979) is a systematic dismantling of aesthetic disinterestedness. He demonstrates empirically that aesthetic taste is socially structured: what people claim to find beautiful correlates almost perfectly with their class position and educational capital. The "pure gaze" — the ability to appreciate art for its formal qualities alone — is not a natural faculty; it is a privilege conferred by cultural training, and its cultivation is a mechanism of class reproduction. The art field (gallerists, critics, curators, collectors, auction houses) is a "field" in his technical sense: a structured space of positions where agents compete over the definition of legitimate culture.

"Taste classifies, and it classifies the classifier."
Applied to the Dataset
The Christie's Post-War & Contemporary Day Sale is Bourdieu's field in operation. The auction house, the specialist departments, the catalogue essays, the estimate ranges — all are instruments for maintaining the boundaries of legitimate taste. An artist who appears at Christie's has been consecrated. The 55% above-estimate rate shows the field currently in an expansionary phase: more buyers competing for the same positions.

Part IV

The Auction as Social Structure

What the Christie's dataset reveals when read through Bourdieu's field theory, Benjamin's aura, and the actual distribution of sale performance across 300 lots.

I.
The Market is Not Irrational — It is Differently Rational

A Longo drawing selling at 14.7× estimate looks like a market failure by the auction house's own calibration. But the estimate is set to frame bidding within an accessible range; exceeding it is not an error. The estimate is not a prediction but a starting bid with social authority. What the data shows is that the art market's "irrationality" (random outperformance, skewed distributions) is actually rationality operating in a different register — one in which social positioning, collector rivalry, and speculative momentum are all priced in, just not by the house's spreadsheet.

II.
Aura is Manufactured at Scale

Benjamin thought aura was irreplaceable once eroded. The auction house demonstrates otherwise: it reconstructs aura through provenance narratives ("from a distinguished private collection"), condition reports, scholarly catalogue essays, and the physical staging of the auction itself. The Christie's catalogue for a Pollock lot includes more apparatus — illustration, biography, exhibition history, literature citations — than most academic monographs. The effect is not decorative. Each textual element adds to the lot's "aura quotient" which is directly reflected in the hammer price.

III.
Abstract Expressionism Dominates for Structural Reasons

The five most valuable artists in the dataset (Frankenthaler, Pollock, Mitchell, Motherwell, Sam Francis) are all first- or second-generation Abstract Expressionists. This is not a coincidence. AbEx's dominance reflects: (1) institutional consecration by MoMA from the 1950s onward; (2) post-war American economic expansion that created the collectors who bought the first generation; (3) the movement's successful absorption into a canonical art-historical narrative taught in universities globally; (4) the physical scale and uniqueness of major works that makes them rare and display-competitive. These are structural advantages, not purely aesthetic ones.

IV.
Women Artists: Systematic Undervaluation, Accelerating Correction

Frankenthaler and Mitchell are the dataset's two highest-performing artists by total sale value. Both spent decades receiving critical recognition without commensurate market returns; both are now commanding prices that reflect revised institutional assessment rather than retrospective discovery of talent that was always there. Alice Neel's posthumous revaluation follows the same pattern. This is Bourdieu's field theory in operation: market position in the art field reflects field-specific capital (critical position, institutional endorsement), not raw aesthetic quality — and the field is currently reassigning capital to women artists who were systematically undercapitalised relative to their male peers.
